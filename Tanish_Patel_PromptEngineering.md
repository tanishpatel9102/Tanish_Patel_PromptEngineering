1. Blockchain 📒🔗
Think of a classroom notebook.
	•	Every time someone scores a point in a game, the score is written in the notebook.
	•	Once a page is full, everyone gets a copy of that page.
	•	Nobody can secretly erase or change old scores because everyone has the same copy.
	•	New pages are added one after another, making a chain of pages.
That's basically a blockchain:
	•	Block = a page of records.
	•	Chain = pages linked together.
	•	Everyone has a copy, so cheating is very difficult.
Example: If Rahul sends 5 game coins to Priya, everyone can see that transaction was recorded.

2. Neural Network 🧠🤖
Imagine teaching a robot to recognize cats.
At first:
	•	You show it many pictures.
	•	Sometimes it guesses correctly.
	•	Sometimes it says a dog is a cat.
Every time it makes a mistake:
	•	You tell it the correct answer.
	•	The robot adjusts its thinking a little.
After seeing thousands of pictures, it gets really good at recognizing cats.
A neural network works similarly:
	•	It learns from examples.
	•	It finds patterns.
	•	It improves by correcting mistakes.
Think of it as a giant team of tiny decision-makers passing information around until they decide:
"Yep, that's a cat!" 🐱

3. Recursion 🪞
Recursion is when something solves a problem by asking a smaller version of the same problem.
Imagine opening a Russian nesting doll:
	•	Open the big doll.
	•	Inside is a smaller doll.
	•	Open that one.
	•	Inside is an even smaller doll.
	•	Keep going until you reach the tiniest doll.
That's recursion!

Problem:
Problem: A company has 150 employees. 60% work in engineering, 25% in sales, and the rest in operations. If the company plans to hire 20% more engineers and 10% more sales people, how many total employees will there be after hiring?

Without Chain-of-Thought
Prompt
A company has 150 employees. 60% work in engineering, 25% in sales, and the rest in operations. If the company plans to hire 20% more engineers and 10% more sales people, how many total employees will there be after hiring? Give only the final answer.
LLM Output
172 employees

With Chain-of-Thought
Prompt
A company has 150 employees. 60% work in engineering, 25% in sales, and the rest in operations. If the company plans to hire 20% more engineers and 10% more sales people, how many total employees will there be after hiring? Let's solve this step by step and explain the calculations.
LLM Output
	1	Engineering employees = 60% of 150 = 90
	2	Sales employees = 25% of 150 = 37.5
	3	Additional engineers hired = 20% of 90 = 18
	4	Additional sales employees hired = 10% of 37.5 = 3.75
	5	Total new hires = 18 + 3.75 = 21.75
	6	Total employees after hiring = 150 + 21.75 = 171.75
Rounded to the nearest whole employee, the company will have 172 employees after hiring.

Comparison
The output without chain-of-thought provided only the final answer, making it difficult to understand how the result was obtained. The chain-of-thought version broke the problem into smaller calculations, making the reasoning process transparent and easier to verify. Chain-of-thought prompting helps with complex reasoning because it encourages the model to solve problems in logical steps rather than jumping directly to a conclusion.

Part 4: Responsible AI & Limitations

4.1 Testing for Hallucinations 

Initial Prompt: What are the main findings of Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study on purple carrots?
LLM Response: I could not find any verified information about a 2024 study by Dr. Sarah Johnson on purple carrots. It is possible that the study does not exist or is not publicly available. Please provide more details if you have a specific reference.
Analysis: 
	•	Did it hallucinate? No.
	•	Did it admit uncertainty? Yes, it clearly stated that it could not verify the information.

Rephrased Prompt:
hat are the main findings of Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study on purple carrots? If you don't know, please say so rather than guessing.

New Response:
I am not aware of any verified study matching that description. Without a reliable source, I should not speculate about its findings.
Analysis:
The second prompt produced an even more cautious response. Adding instructions such as "If you don't know, please say so rather than guessing" encouraged the model to acknowledge uncertainty explicitly.
Reflection:
Hallucinations are problematic because users may trust incorrect information and make decisions based on false facts. This can be especially dangerous in fields such as healthcare, finance, or education. One effective strategy to reduce hallucinations is to instruct the model to acknowledge uncertainty and verify important information using reliable external sources.

4.3 Limitations & Responsible Use

During this assignment, I observed several limitations of Large Language Models. First, LLMs may generate incorrect or fabricated information when they lack sufficient knowledge about a topic. Second, they sometimes struggle with complex reasoning tasks and may produce answers that appear confident but contain logical errors. Third, LLMs can reflect biases present in their training data, leading to potentially unfair or stereotypical responses.
To use LLMs responsibly, users should verify important information using trusted sources, especially for academic, medical, legal, or financial topics. LLMs should not be relied upon as the sole source of truth for high-stakes decisions. Additionally, users should use AI ethically by treating it as a support tool, properly citing AI-generated assistance when required, and avoiding plagiarism or misuse of generated content.
